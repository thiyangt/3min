Why a 3-minute talk?

Many researchers can explain the details. clearly, but struggle to communicate the big picture.

Big Picture

  • Elephant satellite neck belts (or GPS collars) are heavy-duty tracking devices worn around the neck to monitor elephant movements.

  • Using elephant movement data to guide conservation actions and reduce risk to wildlife during extreme climate events like El Niño.

Details

  • Statistics
  • Models
  • Trajectory Analysis
  • Spatial Analysis

Who invented it?

Universities noticed:

  • Research was becoming too technical
  • Public impact was weak
  • Even other academics struggled to understand work outside their field

The 3-minute talk was created as a research communication training tool at the University of Queensland (2008) to help researchers explain complex ideas clearly, quickly, and to non-specialists.

What was the purpose?

  1. Improve research communication by helping researchers explain complex ideas clearly to non-experts.

  2. Improve clarity of thinking by forcing researchers to focus on the core idea, not just technical details.

  3. Bridge academia and the public by making research more accessible, understandable, and impactful for society.

First 15 Seconds Matter

  • Research shows audiences decide whether to listen within seconds.

  • Avoid:

    “Good morning everyone…”

    “Today I am going to talk about…”

Instead, start with a hook.

What is a Hook?

A hook is used at the beginning of a presentation to:

  1. Capture Attention

People decide very quickly whether they want to listen. A strong hook grabs their attention from the start.

  1. Create Interest

It makes the audience curious about your topic and encourages them to hear more.

  1. Introduce the Problem

A hook often leads naturally into the issue or challenge you are addressing.

  1. Establish Relevance

It helps the audience understand why the topic matters to them or to society.

  1. Set the Tone

The hook establishes the mood of your presentation—serious, inspiring, surprising, or thought-provoking.

Hook

  1. Start with a Surprising Statistic

    Every year, millions of tons of plastic waste enter our oceans.

  2. Ask a Question

    What if a single manufacturing defect could cause an entire production line to fail?

  3. Show an object or image.

Managing the 3 Minutes

Problem: 1 min

Solution: 1 min

Impact: 1 min

Tips

Capturing attention quickly

Explaining a problem clearly

Convincing the audience that the solution matters

What should the audience remember after 3 minutes?

A strong 3MT is not about saying everything. It is about making the audience understand one important idea clearly and remember it.

The audience should remember:

- One problem

- One solution

- One message

UQ Rules

  • Presentations are limited to 3 minutes maximum and competitors exceeding 3 minutes are disqualified.

  • Presentations are to be spoken word (e.g. no poems, raps or songs).

  • Presentations are considered to have commenced when a presenter starts their presentation through either movement or speech.

  • A single static PowerPoint slide is permitted. No slide transitions, animations or movement) and is to be presented from the beginning of the oration.

  • No additional electronic media (e.g. sound and video files) are permitted.

  • No additional props (e.g. costumes, musical instruments, laboratory equipment) are permitted.

  • The decision of the adjudicating panel is final.

source: https://threeminutethesis.uq.edu.au/resources/competition-rules

Revise and Practice

A great 3-minute talk is built through rehearsal.

  • Read your talk aloud multiple times

  • Read your script aloud (not silently)

  • Record yourself and listen critically

  • Time yourself strictly—3 minutes is short

  • Practice with people outside your field

    Ask: “Did they understand the main idea?”

Feedback Questions to Ask

  • Was my message clear?

  • Did I sound engaging?

  • Was anything confusing or too technical?

  • Did I finish within 3 minutes?

Deliver with Energy and Engagement

  • Speak in an enthusiastic and confident tone

  • Show genuine interest in your topic

  • Your energy helps the audience stay engaged

Word Choice (What you say)

Use language that is:

  • Simple

  • Direct

  • Easy to understand

Good practices

  • Use short sentences (10–20 words)

  • Prefer active voice

  • Avoid unnecessary jargon

Language to Avoid

  • “Obviously…” (can sound dismissive)

  • Overly formal academic connectors (therefore, hence, thus)

  • Heavy jargon without explanation

  • Long, complex sentences

  • Idioms that may confuse non-native speakers

Words and Phrases to Avoid in Spoken Presentations

Formal academic connectors

These make speech sound written and unnatural:

Therefore

Hence

Thus

Moreover

Furthermore

Consequently

In conclusion (often overused in short talks)

Overly academic or rigid phrasing

These sound like journal writing, not speaking:

“It can be observed that…”

“It is evident that…”

“As previously mentioned…”

“The aforementioned…”

“In the present study…”

Dismissive or alienating words

These can make the audience feel excluded or “talked down to”:

  • Obviously

  • Clearly (when used to assume agreement)

  • As you all know

  • In order to

Filler academic habits

These interrupt natural flow:

  • “Basically…”

  • “In fact…”

  • “Actually…”

  • “So basically what I am trying to say is…”

Instead of formal connectors, use:

Short pauses

Simple transitions like:

“So…”

“This means…”

“What this shows is…”

“Now the problem is…”

Slide Design for 3-Minute Talks: Using Gestalt Principles

Why Gestalt Principles?

The audience sees your slide for only a few seconds before deciding:

What is important?

What belongs together?

1. Figure–Ground Principle

People naturally separate:

The main object (figure)

The background (ground)

2. Similarity Principle

Objects with similar:

Shape

Colour

Size

3. Continuity Principle

People naturally follow lines, paths, and directions.

Design Tip

Use arrows, pathways, or visual flow to guide attention.
